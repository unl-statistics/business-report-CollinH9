Appendix A — Draft: Data Documentation

A.1 1) Gulf Water Temperature Data (Dauphin Island, AL)

A.1.1 Dataset

Primary dataset: NOAA National Data Buoy Center (NDBC) station DPHA1 US Department of Commerce (n.d.) Variable of interest: WTMP = Water Temperature (degrees Celsius)

A.1.2 Who collected the data

The data was collected and maintained by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), specifically the National Data Buoy Center (NDBC).

A.1.3 Why the data was collected

NDBC stations provide operational marine observations to support weather forecasting, ocean monitoring, navigation safety, and climate research.

A.1.4 What the data is about

The dataset contains time-series oceanographic observations from station DPHA1 located near Dauphin Island, Alabama (30.251°N, 88.078°W).

Key variables relevant to this project:

  • YY, MM, DD, hh, mm — date and time components
  • WTMP (°C) — water temperature

Other available variables include air temperature, wind speed, wind direction, pressure, and tide height.

A.1.5 When the data was collected

The station provides multi-year historical observations. Data are typically recorded at 30-minute or hourly intervals. Historical records extend back multiple decades, allowing trend analysis.

A.1.6 Where the data was collected

Dauphin Island Sea Lab station (DPHA1), Alabama, Gulf of Mexico.

A.1.7 How the data was collected

Water temperature is measured using in-situ oceanographic sensors mounted at the monitoring station. NOAA maintains and calibrates these instruments to meet operational standards.

A.1.8 Structure of the data

The dataset is provided as a plain text or downloadable table format. Each row represents a timestamped observation. Columns represent meteorological and oceanographic variables.

A.1.9 Formatting decisions and missing values

Missing values are encoded using sentinel values such as 999, 99.0, or blank entries. These must be cleaned before analysis.

Water temperature is recorded in degrees Celsius and will need to be converted to Fahrenheit to evaluate the 85°F operational threshold.

A.1.10 Data validation

Quality checks should include:

  • Percentage of missing values in WTMP
  • Detection of implausible values
  • Identification of gaps in the time series

A.2 2) Electricity Demand Data (Alabama / Southeast Region)

A.2.1 Dataset

Primary dataset: U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) Form EIA-930 Hourly Grid Monitor
Variable of interest: Demand (MW) — actual electricity demand

A.2.2 Who collected the data

The data are collected by the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) through Form EIA-930 reporting from balancing authorities.

A.2.3 Why the data exist

The dataset supports public transparency and monitoring of real-time electric grid conditions, including demand, generation, and interchange.

A.2.4 What the data are about

The dataset contains hourly electricity demand and generation statistics for balancing authorities across the United States.

Key variables:

  • Balancing Authority
  • Local Time at End of Hour
  • Demand (MW)
  • Demand Forecast (MW)
  • Net Generation (MW)

For this project, demand data for Alabama or the relevant balancing authority serving the Gulf region will be used.

A.2.5 When the data were collected

Data are available at hourly resolution and updated daily. Historical data extend back multiple years.

A.2.6 Where the data were collected

Reported at the balancing authority level covering Alabama and the Southeast region.

A.2.7 How the data were collected

Balancing authorities report operational grid data to EIA through automated submissions under Form EIA-930.

A.2.8 Structure of the data

The dataset is available as a CSV file. Each row represents one balancing authority-hour observation. Columns represent demand, generation, and related grid measures.

A.2.9 Formatting decisions and missing values

Some records may include imputed or adjusted values. The analysis must document whether raw or adjusted demand is used.

Timezone differences between water temperature data and electricity demand data must be reconciled before merging.

A.2.10 Data validation

Quality checks should include:

  • Missingness rates by balancing authority
  • Detection of negative or implausible demand values
  • Time continuity verification

A.3 Relevance to Scenario

The NOAA water temperature dataset allows estimation of the frequency and duration of periods when Gulf water temperature exceeds 85°F, triggering reduced operating capacity.

The EIA electricity demand dataset allows assessment of whether these high-temperature periods coincide with peak electricity demand.

Together, these datasets are sufficient to evaluate operational risk over the next five years by estimating exceedance probabilities and examining seasonal demand alignment.
